Overview

This section is the first in a series that introduces additional operations which build upon the Arithmetic operations. These are usually considered Arithmetic operations due to their nature. They build upon the previous operations, hence their separation into an "intermediate" series of operations.

These "new" operations are useful in many different fields of math, such as Algebra, Geometry, and more. These will be revisited later, but it is important to understand these operations early to better contextualize things later.

This first section introduces Exponentiation!

Exponentiation\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Exponentiation}}Exponentiation is most simply repeated multiplication, but in a very particular manner. It involves multiplying a number by itself a specified number of times.

The Base\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Base}}Base specifies the number undergoing the operation. The number of multiplication operations to perform is designated by the Exponent\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Exponent}}Exponent. Finally, the result of exponentiation is known as the Power\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Power}}Power.

An example is:

23=82^3 = 8 23=8

Here, the base is 222, the exponent is 333, and the power is 888.

Interestingly, this would be read as "Two to the power of three equals eight."

Power doubles as the name used for the result, but also the action of putting a number under exponentiation.

There are some interesting properties of exponetiation:

A number to the power of 0 is always 1. That is:

100=110^0 = 1 100=1

A number to the power of 1 is always itself. That is:

101=1010^1 = 10 101=10

These naturally make sense. If you "repeat" multiplication one time, then the expression naturally becomes the number itself; there is no repetition.

The number to the power of 0 being 1 is actually a result of a more complicated analysis of exponents, which will be explored later.

Exponents can also be negative. These signify that the inverse of the number is to be taken. So:

10−1=1101=11010^{-1} = \frac{1}{10^1} = \frac{1}{10} 10−1=1011​=101​

And:

10−2=1102=110010^{-2} = \frac{1}{10^2} = \frac{1}{100} 10−2=1021​=1001​

Inverting Exponentiation

This operation can be inverted in two different ways, depending on what you are trying to find.

If you are trying to find the exponent, then utilize a Logarithm.

If you are trying to find the base, use the Root.

Cool Exponentiation Laws

Let's take a cool example of exponentiation. Say you have some Real Number value xxx, and say it is undergoing exponentiation, like x3x^3x3.

Multiplying Terms under Exponentiation

Say this x3x^3x3 is being multiplied by x2x^2x2. What happens now? Can we simplify this? Of course! Let's take a look:

Therefore, you add the exponents when multiplying terms with the same base.

Important

To really emphasize this: THE TERMS MUST HAVE THE SAME BASE!

This can be generalized as follows:

xm⋅xn=xm+nx^m \cdot x^n = x^{m+n} xm⋅xn=xm+n

Remember this for the future! It will come in handy!

Even More Fun Rules

Layered Exponentiation

What happens if a number to an exponent undergoes exponentiation? That is:

(x3)2(x^3)^2 (x3)2

Well, let's break it down!

(x3)2Starting point.=(x⋅x⋅x)2Break the term in the parentheses into repeated multiplication=(x⋅x⋅x)⋅(x⋅x⋅x)Break this compound term into repeated multiplication=x⋅x⋅x⋅x⋅x⋅xMultiplication is associative!=x6There are 6 x’s now!\begin{aligned} &(x^3)^2 && \text{Starting point.}\\ &=(x \cdot x \cdot x)^2 && \text{Break the term in the parentheses into repeated multiplication}\\ &=(x \cdot x \cdot x) \cdot (x \cdot x \cdot x) && \text{Break this compound term into repeated multiplication}\\ &=x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x && \text{Multiplication is associative!}\\ &\boxed{=x^6} && \text{There are 6 }x\text{'s now!}\\ \end{aligned} ​(x3)2=(x⋅x⋅x)2=(x⋅x⋅x)⋅(x⋅x⋅x)=x⋅x⋅x⋅x⋅x⋅x=x6​​​Starting point.Break the term in the parentheses into repeated multiplicationBreak this compound term into repeated multiplicationMultiplication is associative!There are 6 x’s now!​

Let's look at this again: (x3)2=x6(x^3)^2=x^6(x3)2=x6. Notice something...? That's right, the exponents multiplied together! That is:

(xm)n=xmn(x^m)^n = x^{mn} (xm)n=xmn

Parenthetical Exponents

Exponents may be applied to terms inside parentheses. For instance, let's say we have xxx and yyy, and they are nested inside a block of exponentiation:

(xy)n(xy)^n (xy)n

What does this simplify to? Well, let's work on it:

(xy)3Starting point.=(xy)⋅(xy)⋅(xy)Convert to repeated multiplication!=xyxyxyMultiplication is associative. Parentheses are unnecessary here=xxxyyyRearrange for clarity=x3y3All done!\begin{aligned} &(xy)^3 && \text{Starting point.}\\ &=(xy) \cdot (xy) \cdot (xy) && \text{Convert to repeated multiplication!}\\ &=xyxyxy && \text{Multiplication is associative. Parentheses are unnecessary here}\\ &=xxxyyy && \text{Rearrange for clarity}\\ &\boxed{=x^3y^3} && \text{All done!} \end{aligned} ​(xy)3=(xy)⋅(xy)⋅(xy)=xyxyxy=xxxyyy=x3y3​​​Starting point.Convert to repeated multiplication!Multiplication is associative. Parentheses are unnecessary hereRearrange for clarityAll done!​

This demonstrates the next law, which is that exponents can be "distributed" to each term in the parentheses!

(xy)n=xnyn(xy)^n = x^ny^n (xy)n=xnyn

What about division? What if x and y are being divided, like the following expression? Can that be simplified?

(x/y)n(x/y)^n (x/y)n

Of course! Let's take a look

Consolidate the fractions}\\ &\boxed{=x^3/y^3} && \text{All done!} \end{aligned} ​(x/y)3=(x/y)⋅(x/y)⋅(x/y)=xxx/yyy=x3/y3​​​Starting point.Convert to repeated multiplication!Division is associative. Consolidate the fractionsAll done!​

Therefore, we have seen the demonstration of this next law:

(x/y)n=xnyn(x/y)^n = \frac{x^n}{y^n} (x/y)n=ynxn​

Scientific Notation

Exponentiation can be used to represent very large numbers in an extremely helpful way. For instance, take the (estimated and approximate) U.S. National Debt at the time of writing this (2026.03.22): $39,021,882,900,000\$39,021,882,900,000$39,021,882,900,000. Rather than having to write out this number all the time, it is possible to write the number in Scientific Notation\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Scientific Notation}}Scientific Notation, which allows for improved readability and far less writing.

Writing this National Debt number would be:

3.90218829⋅10133.90218829 \cdot 10^13 3.90218829⋅1013

This essentially "extracts" the "size" of the number (that is, how many digits there are) into the 101310^131013 value. Generally, numbers in scientific form are written as:

m×10nm \times 10^n m×10n

Where mmm is a number (rational, irrational, or integer) that is between 1 and 10 (or, between -10 and -1). Note that this does not include the number 10.

But, scientific notation can also represent small numbers too! Recall that negative exponents allow you to express numbers smaller than 1 (that is, decimals or fractions). You can therefore represent small numbers too! Imagine instead you were trying to represent the average thickness of a human hair, which is  75~75 75 microns (micrometers). This is equivalent to 0.0000750.0000750.000075 meters. You could also represent this as:

7.5×10−57.5 \times 10^-5 7.5×10−5

In order to manually represent a number in scientific notation, you just need to figure out the exponent, since you already know the number you're trying to represent. To figure out the nnn in scientific notation form, you will need to count the number of decimal places you would have to move the decimal in the original number to get to the final number which must be between 1 and 10.

First, let's follow the process to represent the U.S. National Debt in scientific notation. We'll remove the commas to make it clearer how things work. Recall that the commas are not necessary, do not take a digit's place, and are only visual cues for reading the number.

You counted that it would take 5 decimal places to get to scientific notation! Therefore you would simply multiply 7.57.57.5 by 10−510^{-5}10−5 to get back to the original number.
