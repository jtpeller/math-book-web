Overview

Exponentiation determines the result of repeatedly multiplying a value (the base) by itself a specific number of times (that is, the exponent), resulting in the "power".

Logarithms\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Logarithms}}Logarithms can "undo" this operation by discovering the exponent to which a base must be raised to obtain some specific value.

Often, "log" is said instead of logarithm, due to its symbol: log⁡\loglog

Now, a typical logarithm looks as follows:

log⁡base(anti-logarithm)=exponent\log_{\text{base}}(\text{anti-logarithm}) = \text{exponent} logbase​(anti-logarithm)=exponent

The antilog, or antilogarithm\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{antilogarithm}}antilogarithm, is the "power" in a typical exponentiation expression (it is the result of the exponentiation). The base is the value such that baseexponent=antilog\text{base}^{\text{exponent}} = \text{antilog}baseexponent=antilog.

Now, you may see that the base is omitted. In such cases, it is assumed that the base value is actually 101010 (since, numbers are in base-10).

Natural Logarithms

A special type of log known as the Natural Logarithm\textcolor{cyan}{\textit{Natural Logarithm}}Natural Logarithm is a logarithm such that the base is the Natural Number eee (aka Euler's Constant). This is expressed as:

log⁡e(x)=ln⁡(x)\log_{e}(x) = \ln(x) loge​(x)=ln(x)

This is spoken as "The natural log of xxx"

Let's look at some examples:

log⁡10(100)=2\log_{10}(100)=2 log10​(100)=2

This is because the base, 101010 must be raised to the power of 222 in order to have a result of 100100100. But, not every logarithm is so clean. More than likely, your resultant exponent will not be a whole number, such as:

log⁡5(100)≈2.86135...\log_{5}(100)\approx2.86135... log5​(100)≈2.86135...

Unique Properties of Logarithms

Logarithms have some unique properties. These equalities enable great simplifications when working with them. Use these as a reference when working on problems to allow immense simplifications.

log⁡b(M⋅N)=log⁡bM+log⁡bN\log_b{(M \cdot N)} = \log_b{M} + \log_b{N} logb​(M⋅N)=logb​M+logb​N

log⁡bMN=log⁡bM−log⁡bN\log_b{\frac{M}{N}} = \log_b{M}-\log_b{N} logb​NM​=logb​M−logb​N

log⁡b(Mk)=k⋅log⁡bM\log_b({M^k}) = k \cdot \log_b{M} logb​(Mk)=k⋅logb​M

log⁡b1=0\log_b{1}=0 logb​1=0

log⁡bb=1\log_b{b}=1 logb​b=1

log⁡b(bk)=k\log_b(b^k)=k logb​(bk)=k

blogbk=kb^{log_b{k}}=k blogb​k=k
